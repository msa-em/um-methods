## Sulfur as an Embedding Medium

Epoxy resins have been the embedding medium of choice for ultramicrotomy for most samples since their introduction in the mid-1950s, due to their excellent ultrastructural preservation, minimal shrinkage, and reasonable mechanical stability under electron beam exposure [@10.1002/jemt.1070260604]. However, these resins undergo complex polymerization reactions involving cross-linking between epoxide groups, creating three-dimensional polymer networks of great mechanical strength with volumetric shrinkage typically between 2-7% during curing.
Despite their widespread use, epoxy resins present several limitations for specialized applications. The polymerization process generates exothermic heat and involves chemical cross-linking that can potentially alter specimen chemistry and create artifacts through differential shrinkage. Additionally, epoxy resins do not penetrate tissues as effectively as less viscous formulations and may introduce unwanted chemical interactions in analytical applications requiring pristine chemical environments. These limitations are particularly problematic for precious samples from asteroid sample-return missions, where material conservation and analytical fidelity are paramount.
Sulfur-based embedding represents a specialized alternative that addresses these limitations through fundamentally different physical properties. A pioneer in using this method in the planetary science community was Bradley in his 2003 interplanetary dust particle analysis (@bradley_carbon_1993; @10.1016/B0-08-043751-6/01152-X).

The procedure was originally devised to measure energy-loss near-edge structure (ELNES) of light elements such as carbon, oxygen and nitrogen in organic material and enables X-ray absorption near-edge spectroscopy without contamination from the embedding medium [@10.1016/j.jaerosci.2016.02.008]. Previous studies have demonstrated the feasibility of sulfur embedding for biological specimens, but systematic protocols for hard and composite materials remain underdeveloped. Sulfur is a viable embedding medium because it undergoes a simple physical phase transition from liquid to solid at moderate temperature, without chemically reacting with most samples, and can be sublimated in air to leave embedding-media free sections. Sulfur-based embedding offers several potential advantages over conventional methods: chemical inertness toward most mineral phases, low processing temperatures, rapid solidification kinetics, minimal volume shrinkage during cooling, and improved stability in aberration corrected transmission electron microscopy. Sulfur has a high vapor pressure at room temperature; the ultra-thin sections are then placed in a separate vacuum chamber to sublimate the sulfur, yielding TEM specimens free of any embedding medium. 

This complete medium removal is a significant advantage, as it eliminates chemical interference during elemental analysis. Unlike epoxy resins that remain in sections and can interfere with spectroscopic analyses, sulfur embedding provides clean specimens ideal for studying organic materials and light elements where contamination-free analysis is critical, though it remains more specialized than epoxy for routine morphological studies.
This removability property has made sulfur embedding particularly valuable for energy-loss near-edge structure (ELNES) analysis of light elements and X-ray absorption near-edge spectroscopy (STXM-XANES) of extraterrestrial materials, where contamination from embedding media would compromise analytical results [@10.1016/j.gca.2003.10.044; @10.1016/j.gca.2010.05.010; @10.1126/science.1207758; @10.1111/j.1945-5100.2011.01237.x; @10.1186/s40623-014-0156-0; @10.1038/s41598-019-39357-1]. The technique has been applied to the analysis of samples where preservation of original carbon, oxygen, and nitrogen chemical states is crucial for preserving original materials. Furthermore, the absence of polymerization-induced shrinkage in sulfur embedding provides superior dimensional accuracy compared to epoxy systems, making it advantageous for applications requiring precise morphological measurements or when dealing with thermally sensitive specimens that cannot tolerate the exothermic curing conditions of epoxy resins.

### Protocol Advantages
- Reduced contamination risk through elimination of organic solvents and accelerators
- Enhanced mechanical stability during sectioning due to improved particle-matrix adhesion
- Preservation of volatile and thermally sensitive phases through low-temperature processing
- Compatibility with multiple analytical techniques (TEM, STEM, SIMS)
- Improved handling characteristics and reduced sample loss during preparation

### Protocol Limitations:
- Particle size restrictions based on sample hardness
- Potential for plucking artifacts in samples with high mineral hardness contrast
Requirement for specialized temperature control equipment
- Limited long-term storage stability compared to epoxy embedding
- Issues with sulfur reactivity post-sublimation (copper sulfate dendrite)


## Support Grids

### Continuous Carbon Film Grids
Continuous carbon films are thin films of pure carbon deposited on one side of the grid, typically 5-6 nm thick. They are mechanically not as stable as formvar films but show better electrical and heat conduction in the electron beam. The carbon films provide a thin electron transparent support without hydrocarbons present and are electrically and thermally conductive under the electron beam, providing image and specimen stability for high resolution imaging. These grids are commonly used for nanoparticles and viruses.


*Pros:* Better electrical and heat conduction; electron transparent; no hydrocarbon contamination; provides continuous support.

*Cons:* Less mechanically stable than formvar films; can contribute to background noise through inelastic electron scattering.



### Holey Carbon Grids
Holey carbon films have holes of various sizes up to 100 µm and are useful when mesh sizes of standard TEM grids are too large to support the sample. Continuous carbon films can contribute to background noise through inelastic electron scattering, making holey carbon films a suitable alternative. The increased image contrast in the regions of the holes compared to regions of film is beneficial. Classic holey carbon films have randomly arranged holes of different sizes and shapes with lower percentage of open area than lacey carbon film.


*Pros:* Reduced background noise in hole regions; increased image contrast over holes; additional support beyond standard mesh; suitable for biological and materials science applications.

*Cons:* Random hole arrangement makes automation difficult; less open area than lacey carbon; specimens may not be positioned over holes.



### Lacey Carbon Grids
Lacey carbon films offer a lacey mesh structure with a high percentage of open area, with hole sizes varying significantly and extremely thin bars. The random arrangement and sizes of the holes mean these grids are not well-suited to automated data collection.


*Pros:* High percentage of open area; extremely thin support bars minimize interference; good for viewing specimens with minimal background.

*Cons:* Random hole arrangement prevents automated data collection; variable hole sizes make standardization difficult; less mechanical stability due to thin bars.



### Quantifoil Grids
Quantifoil is a perforated support foil with precisely pre-defined hole size, shape, and arrangement. The quantitatively defined geometry of Quantifoil holey film facilitates the use of automation in TEM, which would not be possible with conventional carbon films. The standard thickness for the carbon foil is 12 nm. For cryo-TEM applications, Quantifoil with circular holes is recommended because the round aspect of the holes helps with formation of an ice layer of uniform thickness. Different patterns are available including R 2/2 (2 µm holes with 2 µm spacing), R 1.2/1.3, and square hole patterns for different applications.


*Pros:* Precisely defined geometry enables automated data collection; uniform hole sizes and spacing; ideal for cryo-TEM with uniform ice thickness; multiple patterns available for different applications; reproducible results.

*Cons:* More expensive than conventional holey carbon grids; requires careful selection of hole pattern for specific application; may have residual polymer from manufacturing.
