## Initial Mounting

- Mount the sulfur block securely in the ultramicrotome chuck.
- Apply secure clamping pressure to prevent movement of the bullet in the sample.
- Center the block properly in the cutting area for optimal access.
- Install a fresh glass knife in the knife holder.

## Pyramid Formation

- Identify the area of interest and create cutting geometry that reflects that.
- Set the initial section thickness to 1μm on the microtome controls.
- Position the microscope for optimal viewing angle of the cutting area.
- For optimal material removal and trimming speed, manually rotate the wheel for rough polishing.

**Quality Check**
- Verify that the pyramid has proper symmetry and significant depth on all sides.
- Check carefully for any surface defects or chipping that may have occurred. Confirm the absence of cracks or instability.
- Replace the glass knife immediately if any dulling has been detected during cutting.

## Primary Face Development
- Diamond Knife Setup
	- Diamond knife (35°-45°) for the precision cutting phase.
- Adjust the cutting speed to 0.5-1.0 mm/sec for better control.
- Approach angle: 6-8° to knife normal
- Set the section thickness to 250 μm per pass for gradual material removal.
- Verify knife edge by making test cuts and examining the results for cracks/chatter.

:::{figure} ./figures/fig_24.png
:name: transition_glass_to_diamond
**Critical workflow transition showing the fully trimmed sulfur sample block moving from the glass knife station to the diamond knife.** The polished block face and refined geometry achieved during rough cutting are now ready for production of ultrathin sections.
:::

**Surface Quality Assessment**
- Inspect face under oblique lighting
- Check for uniform surface texture
- Verify absence of chatter marks
- Capture images to document surface quality
- Record cutting parameters used


## Fine Polishing (250-<100nm) and Optimized Cutting Conditions
- Fresh cutting fluid (ultrapure water)
- Feed rate: lower to 250nm-100 nm per pass
	- 250nm → 200nm → 175nm → 150nm → 125nm→ 100nm → 75nm→ 50nm → <50 nm sections
- Allow stabilization between thickness changes
- Monitor for thickness uniformity

:::{tip}
:class: myclass1 myclass2
:name: a-tip-reference
Accidentally touching the middle of the floating sections can destroy sections altogether. Avoid this by working slowly and touching only near the edges and "paddling" the water around the sections to move them.
:::


**Method A: Using a Perfect Loop Tool:**

When the loop is dipped into the knife boat water and withdrawn at a shallow angle, a thin film of water spans across the circular opening. Surface tension holds this film intact, creating a meniscus. A TEM grid placed on this water film is held in place by surface tension, while the grid floats on the water film, dull side up, creating a stable platform. To capture sections, the loop (with grid on top) is lowered beneath the floating sections. Lifting the loop upward through the water surface, the sections remain at the air-water interface. When the loop rises through this interface, the sections settle gently onto the grid surface. The water film cushions the sections during transfer, preventing damage to the sections. The sections drape naturally across the grid bars without compression or distortion. The technique allows precise positioning and gentle handling.

**Advantages:***The perfect loop provides security for delicate sections and saves more sections from potential damage.*

**Method B: Direct Pickup with Tweezers:**
The operator grasps a TEM grid by its edge using fine-tipped tweezers. The grid is held with the coated side facing upward toward the water surface. A stable grip or self closing tweezer is maintained to prevent the grid from slipping or rotating. The eyelash tool is used to gently move the section chain to the desired location on the water surface. Sections are positioned in an accessible area, away from the knife edge and boat walls. The chain is oriented so it will land centered across the grid bars. The grid is lowered slowly toward the floating sections at a shallow angle (approximately 20-30°) and raise the grid to the surface into gentle contact with the sections. Once contact is made, lift the grid smoothly and steadily away from the water surface. The sections adhere to the grid through surface tension as it breaks the water interface. A controlled, even lifting motion prevents sections from sliding off or folding. As the grid clears the water, the sections settle into their final position on the grid. Any excess water is wicked away by touching filter paper to the grid edge.

**Advantages:** *This direct method offers greater control over exactly where sections land on the grid, is faster once mastered, and eliminates the need for maintaining a water film. However, it requires steadier hands and more practice to avoid losing sections during the pickup.*


:::{tip}
:class: myclass1 myclass2
:name: a-tip-reference
Never grip grids with tweezers into film, only clip on the metal frame or the film/samples will be damaged.
:::

- Dry the grid by tapping the edge of filter paper to the grid to wick away excess water, then allow it to air dry completely. 
- Place grids (sample side up) overnight in an oven at 60°C to sublimate sulfur off of the grid. After this process, sample should be the only material on the TEM grids.

#### Critical Success Factors
- Maintain minimal contamination conditions at all times.
- Exercise patience during all manipulation procedures.
- Use steady, controlled techniques for all movements.
- Monitor process continuously under appropriate magnification.

:::{tip}
:class: myclass1 myclass2
:name: a-tip-reference
**Documentation is extremley important before baking and after.** Document every step thoroughly for reproducibility and troubleshooting. The sulfur sections are easy to see in reflected light, but identifying the locations of the sample in the sulfur will ultimately help identify sample locations post-sublimation. 
:::
