Ultramicrotomy of sulfur-embedded samples yielded high-quality ribbons with minimal sectioning artifacts. The optimal particle size range for this protocol was determined to be 10-15 μm diameter, with larger particles (>15 μm) exhibiting increased susceptibility to plucking artifacts after 10-15 successful sections. Particle plucking occurred when mechanical stress concentrated at the boundaries between particles and the surrounding matrix material. This problem was most severe in areas containing hard mineral particles that had very different stiffness properties compared to the matrix. The plucking happened when the cutting process reached the widest part of each particle, indicating that the bond between the particle and matrix wasn't strong enough to withstand the cutting forces at that point. Quality assessment revealed that a majority of sections met criteria for TEM analysis (uniform thickness, minimal compression, minimal chatter), with 20-25 acceptable sections obtainable per particle before plucking onset.

## Conclusions

Despite continuous advances in specimen preparation technology, ultramicrotomy remains irreplaceable for numerous applications requiring thin sections with minimal preparation-induced damage. The technique's combination of high spatial resolution, preservation of specimen architecture, and ability to generate multiple serial sections from a single specimen ensures its continued relevance in contemporary electron microscopy laboratories. Successful ultramicrotomy requires meticulous control of numerous variables, including cutting speed, knife angle, specimen hardness, air circulation, and water level. Even external factors, such as building vibrations, can influence results, necessitating the isolation of the ultramicrotome from mechanical disturbances.
The sulfur-embedding protocol presented here provides a viable and advantageous alternative to conventional epoxy methods for preparing ultrathin sections of both hard and soft materials. The technique offers superior sample retention, enhanced sectioning quality, and excellent preservation of original chemical compositions, making it particularly suitable for precious samples. Key protocol innovations include detailed procedures, precision particle positioning techniques, and systematic quality control metrics. The method's compatibility with high-resolution microscopic and spectroscopic techniques enables comprehensive characterization while minimizing sample consumption.
Future developments focus on extending the application of these procedures, optimizing embedding procedures for specific mineral assemblages and evaluating long-term sample stability under various storage conditions. This protocol contributes significantly to the expanding toolkit for non-destructive analysis of hard and soft material science research. As analytical capabilities continue to advance, ultramicrotomy will remain a cornerstone technique for ultrastructural investigation across diverse scientific disciplines.



### Protocol Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages:**
- Reduced contamination risk through elimination of organic solvents and accelerators
- Enhanced mechanical stability during sectioning due to improved particle-matrix adhesion
- Preservation of volatile and thermally sensitive phases through low-temperature processing
- Compatibility with multiple analytical techniques (TEM, STEM, SIMS)
- Improved handling characteristics and reduced sample loss during preparation


**Limitations:**
- Particle size restrictions based on sample hardness
- Potential for plucking artifacts in samples with high mineral hardness contrast
- Requirement for specialized temperature control equipment
- Limited long-term storage stability compared to epoxy embedding
- Issues with sulfur reactivity post-sublimation (copper sulfate dendrite)
