## Materials and Set Up

**The primary steps of ultramicrotomy are as follows:**
1. Specimen infiltration with embedding medium (herein sulfur)
2. Solidification of the medium with embedded specimen to form a rigid block
3. Block trimming to create appropriate block face geometry
4. Alignment of block and knife in the ultramicrotome
5. Sectioning at precise thickness increments
6. Collection of sections onto support grids
7. TEM or other examination
 

The first step in preparing samples by ultramicrotomy is to mount the sample. In the case of sulfur embedding, the samples are placed in a droplet of molten sulfur, which solidifies around the sample particle, and then the particle encased in solidified sulfur is glued to the front of an epoxy bullet. It is useful to prepare a batch of epoxy bullets in advance, according to manufacturer specifications to create stable mounting substrates. The epoxy bullets provide standardized mounting blocks for the microtome specimens, ensuring secure attachment in the ultramicrotome chuck assembly and consistent alignment during sectioning, while eliminating time-consuming custom mounting procedures. These uniform cylindrical blocks, cast in silicone molds, offer superior specimen adhesion compared to temporary mounting methods and prevent sample detachment during sectioning of hard materials or serial section collection. Sulfur beads applied to the bullet's top surface serve as an intermediate mounting medium, providing enhanced adhesion for specific sample types while allowing easy specimen removal or re-orientation when necessary.

For ultramicrotomy of individual particles too small to move easily with tweezers, a micromanipulator is essential.  For  embedding high-value, one-of-kind samples, we use a fully computer controlled, dual- axis micromanipulator, e.g., the Microsupport Axis Pro micromanipulator, to position the particles in the sulfur.. This instrument allows users to manipulate particles with micrometer-level  control in order  to orient particles at specific depths within the sulfur matrix. Remote manipulation instead of direct handling can reduce the introduction of foreign materials into the sulfur matrix that might subsequently interfere with analytical measurements.

Several alternatives to Microsupport systems are available depending on application requirements and budget. Narishige hydraulic micromanipulators offer excellent manual control and stability for precision work at moderate cost. The ExLo lift-out system provides integrated ex-situ extraction capabilities for FIB-SEM environments. Manual particle picking using fine needles or precision tweezers under and optical microscope remains the most affordable and practical option, for particles > 10 𝝻m. The selection of  the appropriate micromanipulator system depends on the required precision, sample characteristics, throughput needs, and available funding, with each approach offering distinct trade-offs between cost, functionality, and operational complexity.

The Axis Pro has a stable mechanical design that minimizes vibrations that could disrupt the delicate positioning, and the precise control enables consistency in the embedding protocol. To further enhance stability during the embedding process, we fabricated a custom removable stage ([Plummer, 2024](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/citations/20250003593 "Plummer, 2024")) to provide a more rigid and thermally stable platform than the standard microscope stage, incorporating features such as increased mass for vibration dampening, thermal isolation to prevent heat transfer from external sources, and precise leveling mechanisms to ensure optimal working conditions during the critical sulfur solidification phase. This specialized stage utilizes isolation mounts that effectively decouple the working surface from external mechanical disturbances, including building vibrations, footsteps, and equipment-generated oscillations that could disrupt precise positioning during sulfur embedding. 

To provide a thermally stable working surface for sulfur embedding procedures, we mounted a uniformly heated quartz plate (TC-HPQ75x50, 1.1 mm thickness) on the custom removable stage. The plate features a 75×50 mm heating surface with a 70×45 mm optical window and integrated temperature sensor for precise thermal control. Temperature regulation was maintained using a TC-1-100s-24V temperature controller, ensuring uniform heat distribution across the quartz surface during particle manipulation and embedding. The plate's 128×86 mm mounting frame provided stable integration with the removable stage while allowing optical access for specimen visualization and documentation throughout the embedding process. While the heated quartz plate and its efficacy are described in this manuscript, a hotplate and an optical microscope with reflected light observation, can be substituted.

## Particle Picking and Sulfur Embedding
Manipulation of particulate samples, a.k.a. particle picking, requires a systematic approach combining proper equipment, careful technique, and stringent contamination controls. The essential setup includes a stereo microscope with 10-50x magnification and/or micromanipulator with fine positioning controls, clean glass slides, clean picking tools such as borosilicate glass needles, and precise sample mounting stage with previously discussed specifications, all operated within a clean work environment.  If using the heated quartz plate and removable stage, these need to be set up on the micromanipulator stage so that sample can be seen and documented with both a glass slide with elemental sulfur on it, and a separate glass slide with target material sample grains. Sample preparation begins with mounting particle samples, here we use powdered meteorite matrix on clean glass slides, and documenting target areas of particles thoroughly before picking. The heated plate is then gradually heated from ambient temperature to 90°C, then incrementally increased to 114-116°C, while monitoring the droplet sulfur melting under optical microscopy. Complete sulfur melting is confirmed by observing fluid behavior and surface tension characteristics. Heat sensitive samples should be kept on an unheated glass slide, until ready to transfer into the molten sulfur to minimize heating time and temperature.

The particle picking technique involves first locating target particles under low magnification, before switching to higher magnification for precise particle work. Once particles are identified and documented, individual locations can be saved, in case of a programmable stage,  and appropriately calibrated. Next, the location for the sulfur droplets on the heated quartz plate needs to be identified and set as a saved stage position. Approach particles by bringing the glass needle slowly into focus down towards the stage using the finest control settings to avoid overshooting or damage. Contact should be made gently, maintaining consistent magnification and using steady, controlled movements to transfer particles into molten sulfur medium.

Individual particles can be transferred into molten sulfur droplets and positioned below the surface using electrostatic positioning techniques. The arm-attachment functions of the computer-controlled micromanipulator  allow the microscopist to move particles with a glass or tungsten needle to the droplet, and laterally within the sulfur droplet or  deeper below the surface to achieve optimal embedding depth. Particle placement should be optimized to ensure uniform sulfur distribution around the sample while minimizing air bubble incorporation. The crystallization of sulfur over the sample is critical for maintaining precise particle positioning achieved during electrostatic manipulation and creating a mechanically stable matrix that prevents specimen movement during sectioning. Complete crystallization ensures uniform cutting properties across the block face, eliminating voids or soft regions that could cause sectioning artifacts such as knife chatter, compression, or specimen pullout during microtomy. The solid crystalline structure provides the mechanical integrity necessary for ultrathin sectioning while preserving the particle's orientation relative to the cutting plane, which is essential for exposing targeted internal features or interfaces established during the initial positioning process. 

The central placement of particles within sulfur droplets is critical to ensure uniform embedding matrix thickness on all sides of the specimen, preventing asymmetric support that could lead to differential cutting forces and sectioning artifacts during microtomy. When particles are positioned off-center, thin sulfur regions on one side may cause specimen pullout or chipping, while excessively thick regions create uneven resistance that results in knife chatter or section compression. Central positioning also maximizes the number of useful sections that can be obtained from the embedded block, as it ensures the particle remains within the sectioning plane for the greatest possible depth, providing more opportunities to examine different levels of the specimen's internal structure. Additionally, centrally embedded particles experience more uniform thermal stress during temperature changes, reducing the likelihood of matrix cracking or specimen displacement that could compromise the integrity of the embedded sample during storage or sectioning procedures.

Controlled sulfur cooling with the introduction of nucleation points ensures uniform crystallization that prevents specimen displacement and promotes optimal sectioning properties. Nucleation points can be introduced through mechanical agitation or contact with a cool probe to initiate controlled crystallization from multiple sites throughout the sulfur matrix, promoting fine-grained crystal structure that provides uniform mechanical properties around the embedded particle. Without controlled nucleation, sulfur tends to form large, irregular crystals that create internal stress concentrations and preferential cleavage planes that compromise sectioning quality. The timing of nucleation point introduction requires careful consideration - initiating crystallization too early while sulfur remains highly fluid can disrupt particle positioning, while delayed nucleation may result in uncontrolled crystal growth that creates voids or inclusions around the specimen. Gradual cooling combined with strategic nucleation point placement ensures that crystallization progresses uniformly inward from multiple locations, minimizing thermal gradients that could cause differential contraction and specimen movement during the solidification process, ultimately producing a homogeneous crystalline matrix suitable for consistent ultrathin sectioning.

Quality control measures include taking before and after photographs of picking areas, maintaining detailed records of particle locations and characteristics and verifying particle identity under higher magnification in the sulfur droplet before final collection, with success ultimately depending on patience, steady technique, and maintaining contamination-free conditions throughout the entire process.

### Knife Preparation and Setup 
Glass knives are fabricated by scoring and fracturing specialized glass strips, producing fresh cutting edges for each use. We prepare glass knives with  a Leica EM KMR3 glass knifemaker. A knife thickness of 6.4 mm  enables optimal cutting performance for ultrathin sections  for electron microscopy. While economical and suitable for routine applications, glass knives exhibit limited durability. Edge degradation occurs rapidly with use, necessitating frequent replacement. Glass knives are employed for rough cutting and preliminary microtome sectioning as a cost-effective alternative to diamond knife trimming tools during the initial stages of this sample preparation protocol. While diamond knives are reserved for final ultrathin sectioning (50-100 nm), glass knives are ideal for block trimming, semi-thin sectioning (0.5-2 μm), and survey sections due to their disposable nature and adequate cutting performance. This approach allows for aggressive cutting without risk of damaging expensive precision instruments, enabling researchers to remove excess resin, shape block faces, and evaluate sample quality through light microscopy before proceeding to final ultrathin sectioning. Although glass knives may produce more compression artifacts and chatter marks compared to diamond knives, particularly with harder samples, their fixed cutting geometry (typically 45°) provides sufficient quality for preparatory work while maximizing the lifespan of diamond knives for critical high-resolution applications.

Diamond knives feature precision-cut diamond edges and are ideal for demanding applications and final sectioning. These knives maintain cutting quality for extended periods (months to years with proper maintenance) and can produce consistently thinner sections down to 20 nm. Diamond knives are manufactured in various geometries (25°, 35°, 45° edge angles) to accommodate different material properties. As the knife edge angle decreases, such as 35°, the produced sections have reduced compression of sections, and works well for hard, well-infiltrated specimens. Alternatively, a 45° knife edge angle is more durable, making it less prone to chipping. While the edge dulls less quickly, it may cause more compression during sectioning. This angle is better suited for harder materials or semi-thin sections and is common for glass knives, which are more fragile than diamond knives.

Despite substantial initial investment, the longevity of diamond knives renders them cost-effective for high-volume work. Each knife is fitted with a boat—a small reservoir that retains water. Sections float onto this liquid surface immediately upon cutting, where surface tension prevents collapse and maintains section flatness.


## Ultramicrotomy: Sectioning Procedure, Section Cutting, and Collection
In ultramicrotomy, initial rough cuts create a trapezoidal-shaped block face (commonly called a pyramid) as a crucial preparatory step before cutting thin sections. The pyramid, typically measuring 0.5-1.0 mm on each side, trims the embedded specimen to a small, precisely shaped face containing only the sample of interest. This reduced cutting face minimizes extraneous forces during sectioning, reduces section compression, and supports sulfur stability. Rough cutting can be accomplished through manual trimming with fresh razor blades or mechanically operated trimming using the ultramicrotome equipped with a glass knife. The trimming process begins with examining the embedded block under a stereomicroscope to locate the sample. Excess material is then removed from all sides, working progressively closer to the target tissue while maintaining a trapezoidal geometry. The top face (the future cutting face) should be slightly smaller than the bottom, creating a trapezoid when viewed from the side. As trimming progresses closer to the tissue, finer, more controlled cuts are made to avoid specimen damage or sulfur instability.

The trapezoidal block face shape serves several functions: the parallel top and bottom edges allow sections to form straight, aligned ribbons for easier collection; the shorter top edge experiences less resistance than a square face, reducing chatter and compression; and the slight taper prevents the knife from contacting the block sides during sectioning. For electron microscopy, the final trimmed face should be sufficiently small to minimize section area and maximize image quality.

The trimmed block is secured in the ultramicrotome chuck and positioned relative to the knife edge. Alignment is critical and is typically achieved by observing the knife-edge reflection on the block face. Cutting parameters are established, including section thickness, cutting speed, and knife clearance angle. 

Sectioning was performed using a Leica EM UC6 ultramicrotome equipped with a 45° diamond knife (DIATOME Ultra 45) and ultrapure water boat. Sample blocks were initially trimmed using a fresh glass knife chisel to create trapezoidal faces with optimal cutting geometry. During the cutting stroke, the specimen arm advances the block toward the knife in an arc. The knife edge shears a thin section from the block face, which immediately floats onto the water surface in the knife boat. Multiple consecutive sections often remain attached, forming ribbons. Trimming continued until embedded particles were positioned 1-2 μm below the cutting surface. Sections were prepared at cutting speeds of 0.5-1.0 mm/s with feed increments of 50-70 nm. Sequential sections were collected as continuous ribbons on the water surface and evaluated for quality based on uniform thickness, absence of knife marks, and minimal compression artifacts. 

Ultrathin sections are collected by floating them onto the surface of a water-filled boat attached to the diamond knife. As sections are cut, they drift away from the knife edge and float on the water surface, often forming ribbons of consecutive sections. Section ribbons are retrieved from the water surface using fine tools such as eyelash probes or wire loops and collected onto support grids.

An eyelash tool is a single hair glued to a wooden stick or applicator used to gently manipulate, position, and separate ultrathin sections floating on the water surface in the knife boat during electron microscopy sample preparation, taking advantage of the eyelash's natural fineness and flexibility to handle delicate sections without causing damage. A perfect loop is a simple wire tool consisting of a thin wire formed into a small circular loop at the end of a handle. The loop diameter is usually 2-4mm, roughly matching or slightly larger than a 3 mm TEM grid that works by exploiting water's surface tension to create a stable water film across the loop opening.

Standard TEM grids are 3 mm in diameter, constructed of copper, nickel, or gold, with various mesh configurations (100-400 mesh). To transfer sections to TEM grids, a grid is grasped with fine-tipped forceps and carefully positioned beneath the floating sections, then lifted vertically to capture them on its surface. Alternatively, the grid can be approached from above and gently touched to the section before lifting. Excess water is wicked away by touching the edge of the grid to filter paper, and the grid is allowed to dry completely before examination. The sections adhere to the grid through surface tension and van der Waals forces, with the carbon support film (if present) providing additional stability.


### Support Grids
Copper is the most common grid material for routine transmission electron microscopy applications. Copper grids are widely used because they are inexpensive and work well for most materials with different sizes. The material provides good mechanical strength and electrical conductivity, which helps prevent charge buildup under the electron beam. However, copper can react with acidic solutions used in specimen preparation and may oxidize over time. 

For routine TEM use, copper grids covered with carbon film work well in many cases and provide excellent results. The choice of grid material depends on the specimen type, chemical compatibility with preparation procedures, temperature requirements, magnetic properties of the sample, analytical needs such as EDS, and cost. Specialized applications requiring chemical resistance, high temperatures, or freedom from magnetic interference may require alternative materials despite their higher cost.

### Artifact Management

Several artifacts may compromise microtome section quality (Reis, 1975; Hayat, 2000; Dykstra and Reuss, 2003). Chatter refers to a characteristic defect pattern caused by the vibration of the knife or knife-edge that can result in the blade alternately slipping and sticking on the sample, which leaves a characteristic lateral stripes from variations in the sample slice thickness. This defect can be minimized by ensuring that the ultramicrotome is situated on a table with vibration-isolation, and that the sample is fully enclosed in the embedding media, so that the knife has adequate physical support to avoid slip. Accordion-like distortions that compress the sample sections due to resistance during cutting and may be minimized by reducing cutting speed or knife angle. Knife marks appear as linear scratches caused by debris on the knife edge or edge damage. Thickness variations result from mechanical instability or thermal fluctuations in the advancement mechanism. Section loss occurs when sections sink or fold, typically due to electrostatic effects or improper water levels.
